# Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs): The Wound Beneath the Wound

## A Proposed Layer for the Recovery Model

*Launching from the documentary Paper Tigers and the CDC-Kaiser Permanente ACE Study*

---

## Why This Layer Is Needed

The existing eight layers of the recovery model address what is happening in the person (FASTER Scale), what choice is before them (Double Bind), what fuels change (joy, maturity), what they are feeling (Voice of the Heart), how they relate (Relational Wisdom), what they need (Core Needs), how they act (Practical Wisdom), and what atmosphere they live in (Systemic). The Gospel section declares what God did to make all of it possible.

But there is a question the model has not yet answered directly: **What happened to you?**

Not "what is wrong with you" — the model already rejects that framing. Not "what are you doing" — the FASTER Scale covers that. But the prior question: what happened in your childhood that encoded the false beliefs, wired the survival brain, and set the FASTER Scale in motion before you were old enough to choose?

The Adverse Childhood Experiences (ACEs) framework answers that question with data. And the documentary *Paper Tigers* shows what happens when a community takes that data seriously.

---

## Paper Tigers: The Film That Makes This Visible

*Paper Tigers* (2015, directed by James Redford) follows students at Lincoln Alternative High School in Walla Walla, Washington — the last stop for kids who couldn't make it in traditional schools. When principal Jim Sporleder took over, he shifted the school's approach from punishment to understanding. Instead of asking "What is wrong with you?" the staff learned to ask "What happened to you?"

The results were staggering. Suspensions dropped 85% in the first year. After four years, suspensions had dropped 90%, expulsions fell to zero, and graduation rates increased five-fold. The school didn't lower its standards. It changed its diagnosis. The behavior wasn't the kid. The behavior was a symptom of what had happened to the kid.

The film follows students who carry ACE scores of 6, 7, 8 — numbers that statistically predict addiction, incarceration, chronic illness, and early death. And it shows what happens when one caring adult, one trauma-informed environment, one community that understands the science of adversity decides to meet those students where they are instead of punishing them for where they've been.

The science teacher, Erik Gordon, taught his students about ACEs in the classroom — giving them language for what their bodies and brains had been doing their whole lives. Students who had never understood why they couldn't sit still, couldn't trust, couldn't stop fighting, suddenly had a framework: *this is what happened to me, and this is what it did to my brain.* That framework — the awareness itself — became the first step toward change.

This is what the recovery model needs to name explicitly. The ACE Study is the empirical ground beneath the theological and psychological architecture we've already built.

## The ACE Study: What the Research Shows

The Adverse Childhood Experiences Study was conducted by Dr. Vincent Felitti (Kaiser Permanente, San Diego) and Dr. Robert Anda (CDC) in the mid-1990s. Over 17,000 participants — mostly middle-class, college-educated, with health insurance — were surveyed about ten categories of childhood adversity experienced before age 18.

### The Ten ACE Categories

**Abuse:**
1. Emotional abuse — repeated humiliation, threats, verbal degradation
2. Physical abuse — hitting, beating, kicking, burning
3. Sexual abuse — any sexual contact or conduct by an older person

**Neglect:**
4. Emotional neglect — no one made you feel loved, important, or special; family didn't look out for each other
5. Physical neglect — not enough to eat, dirty clothes, no one to protect you; parents too drunk or high to care for you

**Household Dysfunction:**
6. Mother treated violently — pushed, grabbed, slapped, had things thrown at her, kicked, bitten, hit, threatened with weapon
7. Substance abuse in the household — living with a problem drinker, alcoholic, or drug user
8. Mental illness in the household — living with someone who was depressed, mentally ill, or who attempted suicide
9. Parental separation or divorce
10. Incarcerated household member — a household member went to prison

Each "yes" adds one point to the ACE score (0–10).

### The Dose-Response Relationship

The study's most devastating finding is the dose-response relationship: the higher the ACE score, the greater the risk for virtually every major health and social problem in adulthood. This is not a correlation with poverty or lack of education — the study population was predominantly white, middle-class, and college-educated. The relationship is between childhood adversity and adult outcomes, regardless of socioeconomic status.

**ACE Score of 4 or higher compared to ACE Score of 0:**
- 2x the risk of smoking
- 2x the risk of heart disease
- 4x the risk of emphysema or chronic bronchitis
- 4.6x the risk of depression
- 12x the risk of attempted suicide
- 7x the risk of alcoholism
- 10x the risk of injection drug use

**Additional findings:**
- 85% of prison inmates have significant childhood trauma
- 27% of hospital visits can be traced to causes linked to childhood adversity
- ACEs are linked to autoimmune disease, cancer, chronic pain, and immune disorders
- The effects are cumulative and lifelong — they do not resolve with time alone

### The Mechanism: Toxic Stress

The pathway from childhood adversity to adult destruction is not moral weakness. It is neurobiological. Chronic, unpredictable stress in childhood — toxic stress — changes the developing brain:

- The **amygdala** (threat detection) becomes hyperactive — the alarm system is permanently turned up
- The **prefrontal cortex** (executive function, impulse control, decision-making) is underdeveloped — the brake system is weak
- The **hippocampus** (memory, learning, context) is impaired — the ability to distinguish past threat from present safety is compromised
- The **stress response system** (HPA axis, cortisol, adrenaline) is dysregulated — the body stays in fight, flight, or freeze even when the threat is gone
- **Epigenetic changes** alter gene expression — the effects can be passed to subsequent generations

The body keeps the score. The adrenals don't lie. The limbic system encoded with false beliefs during childhood adversity is not choosing to be broken. It was *shaped* by what happened — and it will continue operating from that shaping until something intervenes at the level where the damage was done.

## How ACEs Connect to Every Layer of the Model

### The Gospel — What Makes Any of This Possible
ACEs are the empirical description of what sin does to children through systems. The promise plan says "I will be your God, you will be my people, I will dwell among you." ACEs are what happens when that promise is broken at the human level — when parents who were supposed to protect, provide, and give identity instead abuse, neglect, and abandon. The Gospel is the declaration that God enters the place of that specific damage and does what no therapeutic intervention can do alone: creates a new heart.

### Layer 1 — Systemic Atmosphere (Friedman)
ACEs are produced by chronically anxious systems — families where non-anxious presence is absent, where reactivity replaces responsiveness, where the failure of nerve in the adults lands on the children. The child in a high-ACE household is living in what Friedman would call a system with no self-differentiated leader. The anxiety of the system is being absorbed by the most vulnerable members.

### Layer 2 — FASTER Scale (Dye)
A high ACE score means the person enters adulthood with the FASTER Scale already in motion. They didn't choose Forgetting Priorities — priorities were never established. They didn't drift into Anxiety — anxiety was the air they breathed from birth. Speeding Up, Ticked Off, Exhausted — these are not moral failures. They are the predictable neurobiological consequences of a brain shaped by toxic stress. The FASTER Scale for a high-ACE individual is not a descent from Restoration. It is the starting condition. Recovery means reaching a Restoration they may never have experienced.

### Layer 3 — The Double Bind (Genesis Process)
The Double Bind assumes a choice between the easier path (self-reliance, coping) and the harder path (surrender, vulnerability, faith). For the high-ACE individual, the harder path is not just hard — it is *neurologically terrifying*. Vulnerability was the thing that got them hurt. Trust was the thing that was betrayed. Surrender feels like death because, in their childhood experience, letting your guard down was when the damage happened. Understanding ACEs explains why the Double Bind is so difficult for some people and apparently easy for others. It is not a matter of willpower. It is a matter of what the brain learned when it was being formed.

### Layer 4 — Maturity & Brain Skills (Wilder)
ACEs directly damage the brain's capacity for joy, relational connection, and return to quiet. Wilder's maturity stages (infant, child, adult, parent, elder) require attuned caregivers at each stage. ACEs represent the systematic disruption of attunement. The child who was abused did not receive joy-fuel. The child who was neglected did not develop the Quiet skill. The child who lived in chaos did not build the capacity to act like themselves under stress. ACEs are the empirical explanation for Wilder's "maturity gap" — the gap between chronological age and relational capacity.

### Layer 5 — Voice of the Heart (Dodd)
ACEs teach children that feelings are dangerous. Fear led to punishment, not wisdom. Anger led to violence, not passion. Sadness led to mockery, not comfort. Loneliness led to further abandonment, not intimacy. The high-ACE individual has learned — at the limbic level, not the cognitive level — that feeling honestly will make things worse. Dodd's framework requires the permission to feel. ACEs are the reason that permission was revoked.

### Layer 6 — Relational Wisdom (Sande)
The 3D awareness circle (God-aware, self-aware, other-aware) requires a baseline of safety. The high-ACE individual may have never experienced relational safety. God-awareness is distorted by a picture of God shaped by abusive or absent fathers. Self-awareness is replaced by hypervigilance. Other-awareness is replaced by threat assessment. The six skills collapse not because of moral failure but because the neurological substrate was never built.

### Layer 7 — Core Needs (Koch)
ACEs are the systematic assault on all five core needs:
- **Security** ("Am I safe?") — No. The home was not safe.
- **Identity** ("Who am I?") — You are what was done to you, or you are invisible.
- **Belonging** ("Who wants me?") — No one, or only conditionally.
- **Purpose** ("Why am I here?") — To absorb the system's anxiety, or to not exist.
- **Competence** ("What can I do?") — Survive. That is all.

The false beliefs the Genesis Process uncovers — "I am worthless," "I am alone," "I must perform to be loved" — are not random. They were installed by specific childhood experiences. The ACE score is a rough measure of how many false beliefs were encoded and how deeply.

### Layer 8 — Practical Wisdom & Boundaries (Cloud)
Cloud's nine principles assume a capacity to act from a stable identity. "Dig It Up" requires the courage to face what is buried. "Pull the Tooth" requires the ability to tolerate pain. "Play the Movie" requires a prefrontal cortex that can think in trajectories. "Upset the Right People" requires the security to survive disapproval. ACEs weaken every one of these capacities. The high-ACE individual is not lacking wisdom. They are lacking the neurological infrastructure to execute it. Recovery provides not just the wisdom but the brain development to carry it out — and that development takes time, relationship, and the kind of patience that only the Spirit of God can sustain.

### The VIM Architecture (Willard)
Everyone already has a VIM — but the VIM installed by ACEs is a survival VIM:
- **Vision:** The world is dangerous. People will hurt you. You are on your own.
- **Intention:** Protect yourself at all costs. Trust no one. Stay in control.
- **Means:** Hypervigilance, substances, withdrawal, performance, anger, numbing.

Recovery replaces this with the Gospel VIM. But the replacement is not instantaneous. The brain must be gradually retrained — through safe relationships, through the Immanuel Journey, through the daily practice of "Would you father me today?" — to believe that the new VIM is trustworthy. Limbic lag is real. The body remembers what the mind is learning to release.

## The Paradigm Shift: From "What Is Wrong With You?" to "What Happened to You?"

This is the shift that *Paper Tigers* documents and that this layer brings to the model. The old paradigm — the one that dominates most churches, most schools, most families, most counseling offices — asks: *What is wrong with you? Why can't you just stop? Why won't you change? Why do you keep going back?*

The ACE-informed paradigm asks: *What happened to you? When did this start? What was your home like? Who was supposed to protect you, and what did they do instead?*

This is not an excuse. It is a diagnosis. Understanding what happened does not remove responsibility for what happens next. But it changes the posture of the helper from judge to healer, from punisher to restorer, from frustrated to compassionate. It is the shift from Proverbs 18:13 — "If one gives an answer before he hears, it is his folly and shame" — to Proverbs 20:5 — "The purpose in a man's heart is like deep water, but a man of understanding will draw it out."

And it is profoundly biblical. Jesus did not say to the woman at the well, "What is wrong with you?" He said, "Go, call your husband" — and then, when her story came out, He met her in it. He did not say to the man born blind, "Who sinned, this man or his parents?" He said, "Neither" — and then He healed him. The question is always: what happened, and what is God doing about it?

---

## Where This Layer Sits

If added to the model, this layer would sit beneath all the others — as the **ground floor**, the empirical foundation that explains *why* the FASTER Scale begins where it does for many people, *why* the Double Bind is so terrifying, *why* the maturity gap exists, *why* the core needs are so deeply unmet.

It does not replace any existing layer. It explains the soil conditions in which all of them operate. A person with an ACE score of 0 and a person with an ACE score of 7 may both be on the FASTER Scale — but they are standing on very different ground, and the work required to reach Restoration is profoundly different.

Understanding ACEs gives the helper — the counselor, the pastor, the friend, the spouse — the compassion to meet people where they actually are, not where we think they should be.

In *The Impact of Early Life Trauma on Health and Disease: The Hidden Epidemic*, edited by Ruth A. Lanius, Eric Vermetten, and Clare Pain. Cambridge University Press, 2010.

— Dr. Burke Harris (California's first Surgeon General) makes the ACE research accessible and personal. She traces the pathway from childhood adversity to adult disease and describes what trauma-informed care looks like in practice.

**van der Kolk, Bessel.** *The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma.* New York: Penguin, 2014.
— Already in the reading list. The neuroscience of how trauma reshapes the brain and body — the mechanism behind the ACE Study's dose-response findings.

**Perry, Bruce D., and Oprah Winfrey.** *What Happened to You? Conversations on Trauma, Resilience, and Healing.* New York: Flatiron Books, 2021.
— The paradigm shift stated as a question. Perry's neurosequential model of development provides the clinical framework for understanding how ACEs alter brain architecture.

***Paper Tigers.*** Directed by James Redford. KPJR Films, 2015. Documentary film.
— The film that makes all of this visible. Available for community screenings. Lincoln High School in Walla Walla, WA, as the model for what trauma-informed community looks like in practice.
