# Paul, the Old Testament, and the Making of a New Cultural Artifact

*A reflection on how Paul's letters shaped — and reshaped — the tradition he loved*

---

## Paul's Dependence on the Old Testament in Romans

Romans is arguably Paul's most Old Testament–saturated letter. He quotes or directly alludes to the Hebrew Scriptures roughly 60+ times, and the density is remarkable — entire sections of the argument are built on chains of OT citations.

Some key examples of how structurally dependent he is:

**Romans 1–4** builds the case for universal sinfulness and justification by faith almost entirely through OT texts. Romans 3:10–18 is a *catena* — a chain of quotations stitched together from Psalms, Ecclesiastes, and Isaiah. His argument about Abraham in chapter 4 is essentially a midrashic reading of Genesis 15:6 — "Abraham believed God, and it was credited to him as righteousness." Without Genesis, that argument simply doesn't exist.

**Romans 9–11**, which deals with Israel's place in God's plan, is the most OT-dense section of any Pauline letter. He draws from Exodus, Hosea, Isaiah, Deuteronomy, 1 Kings, and the Psalms in rapid succession. His argument is unintelligible without the OT — it's not decoration, it's the load-bearing structure.

**Romans 15:7–13** closes the body of the letter with four consecutive OT quotations from the Law, Prophets, and Writings to make the case that Gentile inclusion was always God's intention.

The short answer for Romans: Paul is *extremely* dependent on the OT. It isn't supplementary — it's the evidentiary foundation.

## Paul's Persuasive Habits Across His Letters

Looking at Acts and the other epistles, a clear pattern emerges about how Paul argued depending on his audience:

**With Jewish audiences**, his default method was synagogue-style scriptural argumentation. Acts describes him in Thessalonica "reasoning from the Scriptures, explaining and proving that the Christ had to suffer and rise from the dead" (Acts 17:2–3). In Pisidian Antioch (Acts 13), his sermon is essentially a retelling of Israel's history culminating in Jesus. This is classical Jewish persuasion — you win the argument by showing your interpretation of the shared text is more coherent.

**With Gentile audiences**, he shifted significantly. The Areopagus speech in Acts 17 is the clearest example — no OT quotations at all. Instead he quotes Greek poets (Aratus and possibly Epimenides), appeals to natural theology, and reasons from observable reality. He meets people on their own intellectual ground.

**In his letters to mixed or Gentile churches**, he blends approaches. Galatians uses heavy OT argumentation (the Hagar/Sarah allegory, the Abraham material) even though the audience is largely Gentile — because the issue at stake was *how to read the OT itself*. First Corinthians, by contrast, uses far less direct OT citation and relies more on apostolic authority, appeals to reason, and community norms.

This tells us Paul was a rhetorically sophisticated communicator who adapted his method to context — what classical rhetoric would call *audience accommodation*. He wasn't rigidly tied to one persuasive mode.

## How Much of Jesus' Teaching Appears in Paul

This is where it gets really interesting, because the answer is: *surprisingly little direct quotation, but substantial thematic overlap.*

**Direct citations of Jesus are rare.** Paul explicitly quotes Jesus only a handful of times across all his letters. The clearest examples are the Lord's Supper tradition in 1 Corinthians 11:23–25, the teaching on divorce in 1 Corinthians 7:10–11, and the principle that "the worker deserves his wages" in 1 Corinthians 9:14 (paralleling Luke 10:7). In 1 Thessalonians 4:15 he appeals to "the Lord's own word" about the return of Christ. That's a remarkably short list for someone writing thousands of words about the faith.

**But the thematic echoes are pervasive.** Consider these parallels:

- Paul's teaching on love fulfilling the law (Romans 13:8–10, Galatians 5:14) closely mirrors Jesus' summary of the law in Matthew 22:37–40.
- His teaching on not repaying evil for evil (Romans 12:17–21) parallels the Sermon on the Mount.
- His instructions on paying taxes to governing authorities (Romans 13:6–7) echo "render unto Caesar."
- His teaching about food and stumbling blocks (Romans 14) resonates with Jesus' teaching on clean and unclean.
- The "strong bearing with the weak" theme (Romans 15:1) maps onto Jesus' servant-leadership ethic.

**What this tells us about his teaching character:**

Paul's approach was not to function as a reporter of Jesus' sayings. He operated as a theologian and interpreter, working out the implications of Jesus' death and resurrection rather than cataloguing his teaching. Several things explain this:

First, Paul's audience context was different. He was writing to specific churches about specific problems, not compiling a teaching manual. He drew on whatever authority was most effective for the argument at hand.

Second, Paul's theological center of gravity was the cross and resurrection, not Jesus' Galilean ministry. He says as much in 1 Corinthians 2:2 and 2 Corinthians 5:16. This doesn't mean he was ignorant of Jesus' teaching — the echoes are too consistent for that — but he processed it through a particular theological lens.

Third, the oral tradition was still alive. Paul's audiences likely knew Jesus' teachings through community catechesis. He didn't need to repeat what they'd already been taught — he needed to help them apply it.

## Paul's Letters as Cultural Artifacts — Andy Crouch's Lens

Andy Crouch's central insight in *Culture Making* is that every new cultural artifact reshapes the horizon of possibility — it makes some things newly possible or easier, while simultaneously making other things harder or pushing them further from view. This isn't a flaw in the artifact; it's simply what artifacts do. A new highway makes travel faster and commerce easier, but it makes the quiet rural landscape harder to access and the old walking paths fade from memory.

Paul's letters function exactly this way. When they entered the life of the early church, they were genuinely new cultural artifacts — theological documents of extraordinary power that made certain things newly accessible. Justification by faith became articulable in a way it hadn't been before. The inclusion of Gentiles received a rigorous theological framework. The relationship between law and grace got worked out with philosophical precision.

**But here's where Crouch's framework turns diagnostic: what did Paul's letters make harder?**

They made it harder to hear the voice of Jesus as a teacher in his own right, on his own terms. Not because Paul intended this — as we discussed, he's deeply shaped by Jesus' teaching — but because the form of his writing is so theologically systematic and so rhetorically compelling that it becomes the dominant interpretive grid. When a young believer reads Romans before they've sat with the Sermon on the Mount, Paul's categories inevitably become the lens through which Jesus gets read. The parables start to sound like illustrations of Pauline theology rather than the other way around.

Paul's letters also made it harder to maintain a living connection to the OT as its own voice. This is ironic, because Paul himself was saturated in the Hebrew Scriptures. But his use of the OT was selective and theological — he read it christologically, pulling out the threads that served his argument. When later readers encounter the OT primarily through Paul's citations, they get a curated version. The vast narrative world of the OT — its wisdom literature, its lament, its earthy attention to land and body and community — gets flattened into a collection of proof texts that point forward to Christ and then become obsolete. The OT stops being a world you inhabit and becomes a set of predictions that got fulfilled.

## The Young Zealot Problem

The pattern of zealous young believers majoring in Paul as if his word outweighs Jesus is almost a textbook case of what Crouch would predict. Paul's letters are extraordinarily powerful cultural artifacts — dense, quotable, theologically satisfying, and they give the reader a sense of systematic mastery. For a young person hungry for intellectual coherence in their faith, Romans or Galatians can feel like the key that unlocks everything. And once you have that key, everything else starts looking like a lock.

So what happens? Jesus' actual teaching gets subordinated. The Sermon on the Mount becomes difficult — not just ethically difficult, which it's meant to be, but hermeneutically difficult. How does "blessed are the poor" fit into justification by faith? What do you do with "be perfect as your heavenly Father is perfect" once you've internalized Paul's teaching on the impossibility of righteousness through works? The young Paulinist doesn't reject Jesus' words, but they instinctively reach for Paul's framework to decode them, and in the process something gets lost — the directness, the prophetic confrontation, the earthy kingdom-vision that Jesus was actually proclaiming.

The OT suffers even more. It becomes, functionally, a prelude. The Psalms might survive as devotional material, and Genesis gets retained for the creation and fall narrative, but huge swaths of the Hebrew Bible become *terra incognita*. The wisdom tradition, the prophetic imagination, the Torah's vision of communal justice — these fade into the background because Paul's artifact has reshaped what feels essential and what feels peripheral.

## The "Paul Created a New Religion" Accusation

The accusation that Paul invented Christianity rather than transmitted it has been around since at least the Tübingen school in the 19th century, and it resurfaces constantly. Crouch's framework helps us see why this accusation has such persistent intuitive force even though it's historically oversimplified.

It's not that Paul replaced Jesus' teaching — the thematic continuities are real and deep. It's that the cultural artifact Paul created was so *successful*, so dominant, that it effectively mediated almost all subsequent access to Jesus. Most Christians throughout history have encountered Jesus through Paul's theological categories first. When that happens, it can genuinely feel like Paul is the architect and Jesus is the raw material, even though the actual historical relationship runs the other direction.

This is Crouch's point about artifacts: they don't just add to the landscape, they reshape it. The most powerful artifacts can make their own sources harder to see.

---

## The Old Covenant Gets Eclipsed

This is where Walter Kaiser's work becomes so important, and why voices like his have to keep arising in every generation. Kaiser's persistent argument — that the OT has its own theological integrity, its own promises, its own vision of God that is not merely preparatory — is essentially a corrective to what Paul's artifact, as received and amplified by the tradition, has made harder.

Consider what's been lost or obscured when the old covenant is read purely through Pauline lenses. The Torah's vision of justice — economic redistribution through Jubilee, care for the immigrant and widow, the dignifying of physical labor and land stewardship — gets reduced to "the law" as a category in Paul's faith-versus-works framework. The prophets' fierce concern with systemic injustice gets spiritualized into generic calls to personal holiness. The wisdom literature's comfort with ambiguity, its refusal of neat theological systems (Job, Ecclesiastes), gets smoothed out because Paul's artifact has conditioned readers to expect systematic resolution.

Kaiser and scholars like him are doing the cultural work Crouch describes — they're creating new artifacts (commentaries, theologies, teaching curricula) that try to restore what an earlier artifact made harder. They're saying: the OT is not a problem Paul solved; it's a living word that speaks on its own terms.

---

## What This Means for Formation

The practical implication is a question of ordering and proportion in how we form believers. If Paul's letters are the entry point and dominant framework — which they functionally are in much of Protestant evangelicalism — then Crouch's logic tells us certain things will become persistently difficult:

- Hearing Jesus as a rabbi and prophet rather than as a figure in Paul's theological system will be harder.
- Appreciating the OT as a world to inhabit rather than a set of types and shadows will be harder.
- Holding together the earthy, communal, justice-oriented vision of the kingdom with the interior, soteriological focus of the epistles will be harder.
- Recognizing that Paul himself would be horrified at being elevated above his Lord will be harder — because the artifact has taken on a life of its own, as artifacts always do.

The remedy isn't to diminish Paul. His letters are extraordinary and genuinely inspired. The remedy is what Crouch would call making new cultural artifacts — or rather, recovering old ones. Teaching that gives people the OT first, on its own terms. Formation that immerses people in the Gospels before the epistles. Preaching that lets Jesus' voice ring without immediately translating it into Pauline categories. Communities that practice Torah-shaped justice alongside Pauline theology.

Paul himself would have insisted on this. The man who quoted Isaiah and the Psalms more than he quoted Jesus, who wept over Israel and called the OT covenants and promises irrevocable in Romans 9–11, who shaped every argument around the story of Abraham and Moses and David — that Paul would never have consented to be read as a replacement for the tradition he loved. The tragedy Crouch's framework helps us name is that the very power of Paul's artifact made that replacement possible, even inevitable, without anyone intending it.

---

*The relationship between Jesus' teaching and Paul's letters is less like quotation and more like deep internalization — Paul thought with Jesus' teaching rather than simply repeating it.*
